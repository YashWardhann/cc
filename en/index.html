Impact of the pandemic

on migrant workers

Integrating refugees and asylum-seekers with the society & changing perception towards them.

Integration is a two-way process and it concerns all stakeholders’ contributions, which consists preparedness of the adaption to the hosting country from the refugees without having to give up their own culture, and the capability of supporting multidimensional population from the host society with welcome (UNHCR, 2014).

Changing perception towards refugees and asylum-seekers from the society.

Guarantee the right and opportunities of refugees and asylum-seekers to work

Granting refugees and asylum-seekers the right to work by altering the law of refugees if the right does not exist let them do not have to work illegally, together with giving them chance to work by promoting what these employees could contribute to the employers helps to stabilize their income, thereby increasing their living condition, and a better living condition for the refugees favors the public for getting rid of the stereotype which refugees are poor and dirty, and thus they will do anything including violating the law so as to earn a living.

Educate the refugees about local culture and the locals about refugees’ culture

Providing education for the refugees and locals about each other’s culture improves the understanding about the other’s culture and the importance of respecting the other’s culture, with better understands and greater respects, conflicts which are caused by misunderstanding could be lessened, and with less conflicts, these two populations could be merged more easily.

Try not to view refugees and asylum-seekers as a community, but many individuals

When talking about refugees and asylum-seekers, people are used to see them as one, and therefore crimes from the minority of the refugees, let alone the fake one, stigmatize the whole population. However, are everyone of these refugees the same? Certainly no. They are individuals from different countries and culture, even from the same countries and culture, they differ in characteristics and personalities, and it is not appropriate to have the same view on everyone of them, just like in the local, there are also criminals, but not every locals are criminals, so try not to have a distorted view on them, try to judge who they really are with your own eyes.

Learn the official language of the hosting country

In order to communicate with the host community, being able to speak their language is of utmost importance. With this capability, even it is not fluent, it will be much easier to assimilate with the society as it shows the people in the host community that you have the zeal for respecting, accepting, and learning their culture, and you have the sense of belonging to this country, it helps to create a friendly image of yourself in the locals’ eyes.

Be more enthusiastic on improving relationship with neighbourhood and colleague

Neighborhoods and colleagues are the closest local community to refugees, building a good relationship with them is vital for changing their perception towards refugees and asylumseekers and fusing refugees and locals. Starting from simple greeting, helping them when they need, exchanging each other’s culture by inviting them to learn about your culture, for example, cook your hometown cuisine for them, and asking them about their culture, for the simplest, ask about their language.

Concluding Words

Integration is a two-way process and it requires all parties’ efforts, changing perception towards refugees from the society and proving who the refugees really are complement each other, they are indispensable for each other, and it is our responsibility to contribute in this process in order to achieve the best of both worlds.

BACKGROUND

Case Studies on

Migrants

Since the COVID-19 pandemic started, the number of hardships and problems have exponentially increased for the migrant domestic workers all around the world. Due to the lack of facilities and financial resources, they are prone to both virus, and also societal scrutiny. Many countries reported incidents where the migrant workers were directly blamed for being the vectors of the virus.

CASE STUDY

Thailand

Case Study: Thailand

In December 2020, when the claimed second wave of virus infections originated in Thailand, the Burmese migrant workers (working in the shrimp farms in Samut Sakhon province near Bangkok) were blamed. They constituted 80 percent of some 1300 infected. The Prime Minister of Thailand, Prayuth, called them out by mentioning that they snuck in and out of Thailand, and avoided the mandatory quarantine and testing procedures.

This signified the disproportionate impact the pandemic had on the migrant workers by bringing out the truth about the social exclusion of them in the Thai Society and their precarious working conditions.

Detecting the correct source of the virus is very difficult when the number of infected cases is large, but blaming groups and marginalized communities is relatively easy.

CASE STUDY

Singapore

Case Study: Singapore

In April 2020, COVID clusters were identified in the dormitories of Migrant Workers in Singapore. The number of cases rose to 2689, 60 percent of the cases. The manpower minister said that these workers might indulge in socializing activities during their off days, which might be the probable reason for such a rise.

However, we need to understand that these mega dormitories house 1000 workers each. They obviously live in close proximities to each other. They are provided with bunk beds in poorly ventilated rooms (12-20 in one room). The condition of the kitchens is also not upto the mark, as pests can be spotted regularly, and the overflowing urinal is also common, making the risk of spreading infection ideal. NOt only this, but since these workers are fined if they do not show up for work, so they also work, despite being ill. To counter this, the city adopted the strategy to create a lockdown like situation in these dormitories, under which workers could not leave nor enter these facilities.

This case study is an example how the lifestyle of migrant workers degraded during the pandemic.

CASE STUDY

Texas

Case Study: Texas

On March 4 2021, Texas Governor Greg Abbott tweeted “The Biden Administration is recklessly releasing hundreds of illegal immigrants who have COVID into Texas communities. ” He further took to Talk Radio and Cable news blaming the immigrants for the spread of COVID-19 among the Texas Community. Doesn’t matter if the governor wanted to take a jab on the Bidden administration as this did shift the blame on the immigrants.

Looking at the numbers, about 6.3% of immigrants have tested positive for the virus. Cameron County, where Brownsville is, has a 13.8% positivity rate. Even though the immigrant positivity rate is quite low, still they were being considered as the importers of the virus.

Community leaders in El Paso (which is another border city) believe that there as there is a pandemic going on, there definitely will be Covid Positive Immigrants because the virus doesn’t discriminate but the numbers show the low positivity rate of the immigrants and it’s unfair to blame the immigrants for the surge in cases. Comments like these only fuel hatred among the general population for the immigrants.

GUIDELINES

Guidelines for

Policy Makers

Housing

Ensure access to temporary housing, which allows migrant workers to self-isolate, avoid congestion and evacuate. These accommodation facilities should have sufficient access to health care, hygiene provisions, and sufficient food and water.

Inclusion of Migrant Workers

Ensure equitable access to health care for all migrants, regardless of legal status, ethnicity and identity. Moreover, in order to mitigate socioeconomic inequalities, states must provide access to wage and social protection.

Prevent Stigmatization of Migrant Workers

The fears and uncertainties caused by the pandemic should not be leveraged as a tool to impose repressive policies against migrant populations, and the authorities should prioritize and actively prevent all forms of discriminiation and stigmatization of migrant workers using legislative frameworks and . Moreover, media campaigns and mass media should be leveraged to highlight the positive contributions of migrant workers and combat xenophobia.

Awareness Spreading

The government should leverage community-based networks, community leaders, in conjunction with unions and organizations, in order to disseminate COVID-19 related information. Moreover, measures should be taken to ensure that COVID-19 health activists and health workers should be migrant sensitive, culturally sensitive and linguistically representative. All COVID related information should be translated and be communicated in a simple and accessible manner.

Working Conditions

As lockdowns are being lifted and economic activity is resuming, decent working conditions should be ensured, including safety and health at work, appropriate working hours and staggered breaks to avoid workers congregating in close groups during breaks.

Travelling Arrangements

Due to travelling restrictions and strict regulations, migrant workers who are stranded and are unable to reach their home safety which places them at the high risk of exploitation and human trafficking. Therefore, states must provide the migrant population with access to safe transportation arrangements to prevent this.

Covid Data

The data on the incidence of COVID-19 amongst migrant workers is limited, as the COVID-19 data isn’t disaggregated by migrant status. Therefore, host countries must address this gap in the available data by breaking down, categorizing, and publicizing the COVID-19 data by migrant status.

Health Arrangements

Migrant workers have the fundamental right of access to preventive health care and the right to benefit from medical treatment. Therefore, it is imperative for the host countries to ensure that migrant workers have access to occupational safety and health services.
